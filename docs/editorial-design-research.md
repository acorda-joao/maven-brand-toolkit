## 1. Swiss/International Typography + Photography

### The Core System

The Swiss/International Typographic Style (1950s-1970s) established the grammar that still defines "premium" in graphic design. Its power comes from constraint: everything operates on a mathematical grid, and photography is treated as a structural element — never decoration.

### Josef Muller-Brockmann's Principles

**The grid is invisible infrastructure.** Muller-Brockmann's grids divide the page into columns and rows. Photography occupies whole grid units — never arbitrarily placed, never breaking the mathematical relationships. A portrait might span 3 of 4 columns, leaving one column of white space for type. The precision is what creates the feeling of quality.

**Asymmetry over symmetry.** Swiss layouts almost never center things. A portrait sits flush-left while the headline aligns to the third column. This asymmetry creates tension and visual energy — the eye moves through the composition rather than settling in the middle.

**One dominant element per spread.** Every layout has a clear hierarchy: one large photograph commands attention, supported by smaller elements. When Muller-Brockmann placed multiple images, one was always significantly larger — usually 4-6x the area of the others. This prevents the "everything is equal, nothing is important" problem.

**Photography as geometric form.** Portraits are cropped into strict rectangles that align with the grid. No rounded corners, no irregular shapes, no bleeding beyond grid lines. The rectangle itself becomes a design element — its proportions, its relationship to neighboring rectangles, its distance from the edge.

### Massimo Vignelli's Approach

**Five typefaces are enough.** Vignelli famously limited himself to a handful of typefaces. The constraint meant that hierarchy came from size, weight, and placement — not typographic variety. When combined with photography, this means the type never competes with the image. It frames it.

**The importance of white space as a luxury signal.** Vignelli's layouts breathe. A portrait on a Vignelli page might occupy only 40% of the surface, with the rest being white space and a few lines of Helvetica. The emptiness communicates that the content is important enough to not need more.

**Flush-left, ragged-right text only.** Justified text creates uneven word spacing. Ragged-right maintains consistent spacing, creating a clean left edge that aligns with grid elements and photo edges. This seems minor but it's one of the invisible markers of quality — justified text in a photography layout signals amateur work.

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Establish a visible or implied grid.** Every portrait, every text block, every piece of whitespace should relate to the same underlying mathematical system. For a landing page viewport, a 12-column grid with consistent gutters is the starting framework.

2. **One portrait dominates.** If showing 10+ instructors, one featured instructor should be 3-5x larger than the others. The rest can be equal, but the featured one must command.

3. **Portraits align to grid edges, not to each other.** Don't place portraits based on "what looks balanced" — place them based on which grid columns/rows they occupy. The grid creates balance automatically.

4. **Typography lives in its own grid columns.** Don't overlay text on portraits unless the portrait has intentional negative space (sky, solid background) for it. Give text its own column(s) adjacent to the photography.

5. **Use asymmetric layouts.** The "Learn ___ from humans" headline should not be centered on the viewport. Place it aligned to a grid column — left-third or right-third — with the portrait grid occupying the remaining space.

6. **White space is proportional to quality perception.** The more breathing room around and between portraits, the more premium it feels. Tight grids with no gutters feel like a contact sheet. Wide gutters (24-40px) feel like a gallery.

## 2. Magazine Editorial Spreads

### Vogue / Harper's Bazaar — Fashion Photography Layouts

**The full-bleed hero portrait.** Fashion magazines pioneered the single-image-as-entire-page approach. One face, one body, filling the entire spread. No text on the image except a small folio number. The image IS the content.

**The hero-plus-supporting layout.** A common Vogue pattern: left page is a full-bleed portrait, right page has 2-3 smaller images arranged asymmetrically with caption text. The full-bleed image is always the "establishing shot" (usually face/emotion), while the smaller images are detail/action/context.

**Text wrapped around subject.** When text must coexist with a portrait, Harper's Bazaar often runs body copy in a narrow column that wraps around the subject's silhouette. This creates an organic relationship between text and image — the person's presence literally shapes the layout.

**The opening spread principle.** The first spread of a feature always has the largest, most dramatic image. Subsequent spreads can use smaller images or grids. This creates a "crescendo in reverse" — you start at maximum impact and then provide depth.

### Bloomberg Businessweek — Bold Graphic + Portrait Combinations

**Photography as graphic element, not document.** Businessweek treats portraits like graphic shapes. They might solarize a CEO's headshot, cut it out and place it at an angle, overlay bold geometric type at 120pt across the face. The portrait is raw material for graphic design, not sacred.

**Extreme scale contrast.** A typical Businessweek spread might have a name set at 200pt overlapping a portrait, while the body copy is 9pt. The ratio between largest and smallest element can be 20:1 or more. This extreme contrast is what creates the "bold" feeling.

**Color overlay and duotone as editorial shorthand.** Businessweek frequently applies single-color washes to portraits — a red duotone signals urgency/controversy, blue signals tech/finance, green signals money/growth. This is a powerful tool for unifying diverse photography: take 10 different headshots and apply the same duotone, and they immediately feel like they belong together.

**Bold geometric backgrounds behind cut-out portraits.** Instead of running portraits on white or contextual backgrounds, Businessweek places cut-out figures against flat color blocks, patterns, or bold typography. This creates a collage-like energy that feels editorial and intentional.

### Kinfolk / Cereal — Minimalist Photography-Forward Layouts

**The power of restraint.** Kinfolk spreads often have a single portrait occupying 60% of the spread with 40% white space. The portrait is not full-bleed — it's placed with deliberate margins, making the white space as intentional as the image.

**Natural light, muted tones, consistent color temperature.** Kinfolk's photography cohesion comes from color palette, not post-processing tricks. All photos share a warm, desaturated, low-contrast look. When building a grid of diverse portraits, applying a consistent color grade (lower saturation, slightly warm, slightly lifted blacks) is the fastest path to cohesion.

**Generous margins and extreme whitespace.** Kinfolk might run a portrait at 5x7" in the center of an 8.5x11" page, with margins wider than the image itself. This "island" placement makes every image feel like a museum piece.

**Type as whisper, not shout.** Kinfolk uses small, light-weight type — often 8-10pt serif in all-caps with generous letter-spacing. This prevents text from competing with the photography and creates a quiet, confident tone.

### Monocle — Grid-Based Editorial with Portraits

**The structured feature grid.** Monocle's signature is a dense but ordered grid. A "People to Watch" feature might show 20 portraits in a 5x4 grid, but with these key moves that prevent it from feeling like a yearbook:
- Alternating between studio and environmental portraits
- Some images are tight face crops, others are wider shots showing context
- Every third or fourth image might be slightly larger (1.5x)
- A pull quote or colored block replaces one grid cell

**Color-coded sections.** Monocle assigns colors to sections — yellow for business, pink for culture, blue for design. When portraits appear within a section, the section color often appears as a thin border, background stripe, or category label. This is directly applicable to Maven's category system.

**The "people wall" done right.** Monocle's annual "Forecast" issue has a spread showing 50+ small portraits. What makes it work: every portrait has the same crop (tight headshot, centered eyes), same background (light grey), same size. The uniformity makes the *faces* — their diversity, expressions, features — become the visual variety. The system is uniform so the humans stand out.

### The Gentlewoman — Portrait-Forward Editorial Design

**The anti-glamour portrait.** The Gentlewoman uses portraits that are direct, minimal, almost confrontational. Subject looks straight at camera. Simple background. Natural lighting. No heavy retouching. The result feels honest and confident — the person's presence is enough.

**Portrait as cover element.** The Gentlewoman's covers are almost always a single face, often cropped just below the eyes or at the hairline, with the magazine title overlaying the forehead area. This extreme close-crop creates intimacy — you feel like you're meeting the person.

**The profile spread structure.** A typical Gentlewoman profile: page 1 is a full-page portrait (face, direct gaze); page 2 has text in two columns with a smaller secondary photo (candid, showing the person in context); pages 3-4 might be a single landscape image or a collage of details. The pattern is always: establish the person's face first, then expand into their world.

**Generous leading and tracking in text.** Body text has 150-170% line-height and slightly wide letter-spacing. This creates a sense of space and luxury even in text-heavy pages — the text "breathes" in the same way the photographs do.

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Vary portrait crops to create rhythm.** In a multi-portrait grid, alternate between: tight face crops (eyes to chin), head-and-shoulders, and wider contextual shots. The variation in crop creates visual rhythm even when images are the same size.

2. **Apply a consistent color grade across all portraits.** Before placing headshots in a layout, run them through the same treatment: consistent white balance, similar contrast curve, matched saturation levels. This is the single most impactful step for making diverse photos feel cohesive.

3. **Use a "break" element in portrait grids.** Every 4th or 5th grid cell can be replaced with a typographic element, a colored block, or a pull quote. This prevents the grid from feeling mechanical and gives the eye a rest.

4. **Full-bleed featured portrait + supporting grid.** The strongest magazine pattern: one instructor fills 50-60% of the viewport (full-bleed or near-full-bleed), while remaining instructors appear in a smaller grid occupying 30-40%. This creates both impact (the hero) and breadth (the community).

5. **White space is not wasted space.** Allow 15-25% of the viewport to be breathing room — margins, gutters, empty grid cells. This is the primary signal that separates "premium editorial" from "content grid."

6. **Category color can appear as subtle signals.** Thin colored borders, small color dots, or tinted backgrounds behind names — not as dominant color blocks that overwhelm the photography. Monocle's approach: color as metadata, not as decoration.

## 3. Contemporary Graphic Design Awards — Photography in Digital

### Patterns from Award-Winning Sites

**The "editorial hero" pattern.** The most awarded photography-heavy sites (Awwwards, D&AD, FWA) share a common hero structure: a large portrait (minimum 50vh height) with type placed in the remaining space, never overlaid on the face. The face and the text are two distinct zones with a clear boundary — sometimes a hard line, sometimes a gradient.

**Horizontal scroll galleries over vertical grids.** Award-winning portfolio and editorial sites increasingly use horizontal scrolling for portrait galleries. This mimics the physical experience of walking past framed portraits in a gallery. Each portrait gets its own "frame" (viewport width or near it), and the scroll creates sequence and pacing.

**Cursor-reactive photography.** Sites that win interaction design awards often make portraits responsive to cursor position: subtle parallax, slight rotation, or a reveal effect (hovering reveals color while default state is B&W). These micro-interactions create a sense that the portraits are alive — "these are real people who respond to your presence."

**The "broken grid" over strict grid.** Rather than a perfectly aligned CSS grid, awarded sites use slightly offset, overlapping, or staggered portrait placements. A row of three portraits where the middle one sits 30px higher than the others. This creates designed imperfection that feels curated rather than templated.

**Typography as structural element, not caption.** In awarded sites, the name or headline is often the same visual weight as the portrait — set at 80-160px, positioned as a compositional element rather than a label below the image. The text and image form a single composition.

### Hero Sections with Portraits

**The split-screen hero.** Most common pattern: left half is typography (headline, subhead, CTA), right half is a portrait or portrait grid. The split can be 50/50, 40/60, or 30/70. The portrait side is almost always the larger portion.

**The "behind the text" hero.** Portrait fills the entire viewport as a background. Typography overlays the portrait, but is placed in areas of the photo with low visual complexity (solid background areas, out-of-focus regions, or the space beside the subject's head). This requires either careful photo selection or portrait cutouts with controlled backgrounds.

**The "emerging" hero.** The portrait starts partially hidden — cropped at the bottom of the viewport, behind a text block, or masked by a geometric shape — and reveals as the user scrolls or after a timed delay. This creates a "meeting the person" moment.

### Grid-Based Photo Galleries

**The masonry gallery.** Images of different aspect ratios arranged in a Pinterest-style layout. Works well when you have varied photography (some horizontal, some vertical) but creates an informal feeling that may be too casual for "premium." Best reserved for "browse all" views rather than hero sections.

**The editorial grid.** Strict rows and columns with consistent gutters but varied image sizes within the grid. One image spans 2 columns, another spans 2 rows. A "bento box" approach where the grid cells are consistent but their spans vary. This is the pattern that best combines order (premium) with variety (not boring).

**The single-row filmstrip.** All portraits in a horizontal line, either scrollable or auto-advancing. Each portrait is the same size. Works well for 6-12 portraits. Beyond 12, it feels infinite and loses impact.

### Typography Over Photography

**Rule: text needs contrast or separation.** The three reliable approaches:
1. **Text in the "quiet zone"** — place text where the photo has low detail (solid backgrounds, sky, blurred areas)
2. **Text with backing** — a semi-transparent dark or light overlay behind the text, or a solid text container
3. **Text separated by space** — don't overlay at all; place text adjacent to the image with clear margins

**Scale creates permission.** Very large text (80px+) can overlap a portrait if it's bold enough — the text becomes a compositional element rather than attempting to be "readable over an image." Very small text (12px or under) can sit on any background with a subtle shadow. It's mid-sized text (18-32px) that struggles most with photo backgrounds.

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Use a "broken grid" rather than a perfect grid.** Offset alternate rows by 20-40px vertically. This single change transforms a yearbook grid into an editorial layout.

2. **Make the featured portrait at least 50vh.** The hero instructor should dominate the viewport. Award-winning hero sections give the portrait room to breathe — minimum half the viewport height.

3. **Don't overlay text on faces.** Place "Learn ___ from humans" in its own dedicated space, adjacent to the portrait area. If it must be near the portraits, position it in the upper-left or lower-left where the grid has an intentional gap.

4. **Add cursor-reactive microinteractions.** Even subtle parallax (2-5px of movement tracking cursor) on the portrait grid makes the page feel alive and the people feel present. This is a high-impact, low-effort interaction for awards perception.

5. **Consider horizontal scroll for a "gallery walkthrough" section.** If the page has any scroll at all, a horizontal section where you "walk past" each instructor — seeing their face large with their name and topic — creates a premium gallery experience.

## 4. Art Photography & Gallery Design

### Richard Avedon — The White Background Principle

Avedon's most iconic portraits share one radical choice: a seamless white background. No context, no environment, no distraction. The person's face, posture, and expression carry everything.

**Why this works for multi-portrait displays:** When all portraits share an identical background, the background disappears. The eye sees only the differences between people — their expressions, features, energy. This is why passport photos and yearbooks also use uniform backgrounds, but Avedon's work proves the technique is about *what you choose to foreground*, not about the technique itself.

**Avedon's crop and composition rules:**
- Eyes always in the top third of the frame (not centered)
- Subjects shot from very slightly below, giving them a sense of stature
- Tight crops that include some negative space above the head (never cropped at the scalp)
- Hands visible when they add character; excluded when they'd distract
- Subject always sharp, even in large format — the detail of skin, fabric, hair is part of the portrait's power

### Annie Leibovitz — Environmental and Conceptual Portraiture

Where Avedon strips away context, Leibovitz adds it. Her editorial portraits place subjects in environments that extend the narrative: a tech CEO in a server room, a musician in an empty concert hall, a writer surrounded by stacked books.

**The "character poster" approach.** Leibovitz's group portraits (like her iconic Vanity Fair Hollywood issues) solve the multi-person problem through hierarchy and staging:
- One or two subjects in the foreground, larger, facing camera
- Others arranged behind or beside them, at varying distances from camera
- Some subjects seated, some standing, some leaning — posture variation creates rhythm
- The composition reads like a sentence: main subjects first, supporting cast after

**Scale relationships in group shots.** Leibovitz's group images use depth of field to create layers: foreground subjects are sharp and large, background subjects are slightly softer and smaller. This optical hierarchy means every person is present but the eye knows where to start.

### Irving Penn — Studio Precision and the "Corner" Portraits

Penn's famous "corner portraits" placed subjects between two converging walls, creating natural containment and tension. The architectural constraint forced subjects into revealing postures.

**Penn's approach to uniformity with variation:**
- Same studio, same light setup, same background for an entire series
- All variation comes from the subject — their expression, clothing, posture
- The systematic consistency makes each subject's individuality more visible
- Proving that "sameness of system" amplifies "difference of person"

**Penn's tonal control.** Penn's portraits often have a narrow tonal range — few pure whites, few pure blacks. This mid-tone approach creates a sense of quiet authority. For a grid of portraits, a consistent mid-tone palette (avoiding harsh shadows or blown highlights) unifies diverse photos.

### How Galleries Create "Walls of Faces" That Feel Curated

**The grid with breathing room.** In physical galleries, portrait grids typically use 4-8 inches of wall space between frames. The wall (negative space) is as important as the images. Digital translation: gutters should be 3-5% of the image width, not hairline gaps.

**Consistent framing as a unifying device.** Physical galleries frame all portraits in identical frames — same material, same width, same mat color. The digital equivalent is consistent image containers: same border-radius (or none), same aspect ratio, same shadow treatment (or none). The container is the "frame" that unifies diverse images.

**Alignment of eyes across multiple portraits.** Professional gallery installations align the eye-line of subjects across multiple framed prints. Even when print sizes vary, the eyes sit at approximately the same height from the floor (typically 57-63 inches, center of the frame at human eye level). Digital translation: in a grid of portraits, crop all images so the subjects' eyes sit at approximately the same vertical position within each frame. This creates an invisible horizontal line that brings order.

**The "salon hang" vs. the "linear hang."** A salon hang clusters differently-sized works densely on a wall, creating an organic whole. A linear hang places works in a single row with even spacing. Both can feel curated, but they communicate differently: salon = abundance, richness; linear = precision, selectivity. For Maven's "many instructors" challenge, a modified salon hang (bento grid with varied sizes) communicates abundance and richness.

**Scale variation for hierarchy.** Galleries often pair one large print (30x40") with several smaller prints (8x10" or 11x14"). The large print is the anchor — the one that draws you across the room. The smaller prints reward closer inspection. For a landing page: one instructor at 300-400px width, others at 120-180px, creates this gallery dynamic in digital space.

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Align eyes across the grid.** When placing multiple portraits in a row, crop each image so the subject's eyes sit at the same vertical position (roughly 33% from the top of the image). This creates invisible horizontal alignment that feels ordered without being rigid.

2. **Use a consistent background treatment.** Either remove all backgrounds (cut out subjects) and place on a shared background color, or ensure all original photos share a similar background tone. Mixed backgrounds (some white, some grey, some colored) break cohesion immediately.

3. **Vary portrait scale, not portrait style.** All portraits should feel like they come from the same photo series (consistent lighting direction, similar color temperature, matched contrast). The variation should come from *size and crop* — some tight face, some wider — not from fundamentally different photography styles.

4. **Maintain generous spacing between portraits.** Gutters of 16-32px between portraits at typical screen sizes. Tighter than 12px starts feeling like a contact sheet. Wider than 48px starts feeling disconnected.

5. **One portrait at 2-3x the size of others creates the gallery anchor.** Without a size hierarchy, a grid of equally-sized portraits has no entry point. The eye wanders. One larger portrait tells the eye where to start.

## 5. Film Poster & Title Design

### Saul Bass — Principles of Face + Type Composition

Bass proved that you don't need to show the whole face. His title designs for Hitchcock, Preminger, and Scorsese used:

**Fragmentation as storytelling.** A face sliced into horizontal strips (Anatomy of a Murder). An eye in extreme close-up (Vertigo). A silhouette with no facial detail (The Man with the Golden Arm). The PARTIAL face is more intriguing than the whole face — it invites the viewer to complete the image.

**Typography AS image.** In Bass's work, the title isn't placed "on" the design — it's part of the same visual system. Letters might share the same color as the face, or be cut from the same graphic shape, or be arranged to echo the portrait's contours. Text and image are peers, not hierarchy.

**The power of silhouette.** A face in silhouette (no internal detail, just the outline) is instantly recognizable but graphically bold enough to coexist with large typography. For a multi-portrait layout, mixing silhouettes with detailed portraits creates visual variety within a system.

### Modern Prestige TV Key Art

**Succession** — The ensemble arranged by power dynamics. The family members are positioned according to narrative hierarchy: Logan Roy central and largest, children flanking at slightly smaller scale, peripheral characters in the background. The spatial composition tells you the story's structure before you read a word.

**Severance** — The split identity expressed through portrait manipulation. Portraits are literally cut in half, doubled, or reflected. The key art takes a standard headshot and makes it *conceptual* — the portrait is no longer just "who this person is" but "what this show is about."

**The Bear** — Environmental portraiture. Characters shown in their workspace (kitchen), in action, surrounded by the tools of their craft. The key art says: these are people who DO things, not just people who look good.

**Common patterns across prestige TV key art:**
- Primary character is 2-4x the scale of supporting characters
- Characters arranged in layers (foreground, midground, background)
- A consistent color grade across all characters — usually a specific LUT that gives the whole image a signature look (Succession's cold gold, Severance's clinical cyan)
- Text (show title) occupies a clear zone — either centered at top/bottom of the composition, or integrated into the negative space between characters
- Characters who face the camera communicate confidence/power; characters in profile or 3/4 view communicate complexity/mystery

### Portrait Scale, Crop, and Typographic Hierarchy

**The closer the crop, the more intimate.** A tight crop showing only eyes-to-lips feels intense and personal. A wider shot showing head-to-waist feels professional and contextual. A full-body shot feels distant and documentary. For a landing page, tight face crops create the strongest emotional connection — "this is a real person looking at you."

**Crop variety creates narrative rhythm.** In ensemble posters, not every person is cropped the same way:
- The lead: tight face crop, largest scale, facing camera directly
- Supporting leads: head-and-shoulders, medium scale, slight turn
- Ensemble: smaller scale, more variety in pose and crop
- This variety tells the viewer that the composition is intentional and hierarchical, not a template applied uniformly

**Where the name goes relative to the face:** Three reliable positions:
1. **Below the face** (most common) — feels like a nameplate, formal, gallery-like
2. **Beside the face** (horizontally aligned with the eyes) — feels editorial, magazine-like
3. **Over the body/shoulders** (never over the face) — feels bold, poster-like

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Vary crop tightness across the grid.** The featured instructor gets a tight face crop (eyes to chin). Supporting instructors get head-and-shoulders crops. This creates visual hierarchy through crop, not just size.

2. **Consider partial/cropped portraits at the grid edges.** Portraits at the edges of the viewport can be cropped by the viewport boundary — showing only half a face or cutting off at the shoulder. This implies that the grid extends beyond the visible area, suggesting abundance and depth.

3. **Place instructor names below or beside their portraits, never over their faces.** Text overlay on faces reads as "design template" not "editorial quality." Names below images (like gallery labels) or to the side (like magazine captions) feel considered.

4. **Apply one consistent color grade across all portraits.** Choose a single LUT or color treatment: blue-shifted shadows for Maven's navy palette, desaturated midtones, consistent highlight warmth. This is the highest-leverage step for making diverse headshots feel like one cohesive series.

5. **Use depth/layer composition for the ensemble.** Rather than all portraits in the same flat plane, create 2-3 visual layers: one instructor large in the foreground, 3-4 at medium scale in the midground, remaining 6-16 smaller in the background layer. This creates cinematic depth.

## 6. Layout Principles for Multiple Portraits (10-24 Faces)

This is the central challenge. Here are the documented solutions.

### The "Multiple Person Feature" Spread in Magazines

**Pattern 1 — The Hero + Grid.**
Time Magazine's "Person of the Year" runner-up spreads, Vanity Fair's "New Establishment": one person gets a full-page portrait (the featured/lead), while the remaining 10-20 people appear in a structured grid on the facing page(s). The hero image is 4-8x larger than any individual grid image. The grid has consistent sizing and minimal variation — the uniformity of the grid is a deliberate contrast to the singularity of the hero.

**Pattern 2 — The Staggered Column.**
GQ, Esquire "Men/Women of the Year": portraits are arranged in 2-3 vertical columns with staggered heights. Column 1 might have 4 portraits, column 2 has 3 (starting 40% down the page), column 3 has 4 (starting 20% down). The stagger creates diagonal visual flow and prevents the "yearbook" read pattern (left-to-right, row by row).

**Pattern 3 — The Feature Rotation.**
Each person gets their own mini-spread or section within a larger feature. Readers experience them sequentially, like chapters. For a landing page, this translates to a carousel or auto-cycling spotlight — showing one person at a time with the full grid visible but de-emphasized in the background.

**Pattern 4 — The Cluster.**
Instead of a rigid grid, portraits are clustered in organic groups with varying amounts of space between them. 3 portraits close together, a gap, then 2 more, a gap, then 4. The clusters might correspond to categories or relationships. This creates visual "sentences" rather than a "list."

### How Ensemble Cast Posters Avoid Looking Generic

**Principle: asymmetric arrangement.** Generic ensemble posters place everyone in the same row at the same size. Premium ensemble posters (Ocean's Eleven, Avengers) create asymmetric compositions where people are at different scales, depths, and angles.

**Principle: overlapping.** When portraits slightly overlap (one person's shoulder behind another person's head), it creates depth and connection. No overlap = a collection of separate images. Slight overlap = a composed group.

**Principle: gaze direction.** Not everyone looks at the camera. Some look at each other, some look off-frame, some look at the lead character. Varied gaze directions create internal visual relationships within the composition.

**Principle: tonal continuity.** Every person is lit the same way and graded the same way. If one person has warm golden light and another has cool fluorescent light, the composition falls apart. Lighting/grading consistency is non-negotiable.

### What Makes a Grid of Faces Feel Curated vs. Clip-Art

**Clip-art grid characteristics (what to avoid):**
- All portraits are the same size and crop
- No size hierarchy — every face is equally important (meaning none are important)
- Identical spacing everywhere — mechanical, automated feel
- Mixed photography styles — some studio, some selfie, some environmental
- Different background colors behind different portraits
- Names in the same typeface, same size, same position under each photo
- Perfect row/column alignment with no breaks or interruptions

**Curated grid characteristics (what to achieve):**
- 2-3 portrait sizes in the grid (large, medium, small)
- Size hierarchy: 1-2 portraits are clearly featured/larger
- Varied gutters or at least one "break" element (text block, empty cell, colored cell)
- Consistent photography style and color treatment across all portraits
- Unified background (either all removed/cutout, or all the same tone)
- Typography that varies in scale with image size (larger images = larger names)
- Slight irregularity in the grid — a stagger, an offset, an asymmetric arrangement
- At least one "editorial" moment: a portrait that bleeds off the edge, or a text overlay, or a pull quote

### Rhythm, Scale Variation, Crop Variation, Negative Space

**Rhythm = pattern of large-small-large-small.** Like music, visual rhythm comes from repetition with variation. A grid where every row is [medium, medium, medium] has no rhythm. A grid where the pattern is [large, small, small, medium, small, large] has rhythm. The key is that the pattern is felt, not calculated — it should feel like a considered composition, not a mathematical formula.

**Scale variation rules of thumb:**
- Small portraits: 80-120px (thumbnails — you see the face but not the details)
- Medium portraits: 160-240px (clear face, can read expression)
- Large/featured portraits: 320-480px (dominant, commanding, full detail)
- Use no more than 3 sizes in a single layout. More than 3 sizes looks chaotic.

**Crop variation creates visual texture:**
- Mix 1: Tight face (eyes to chin) — 30% of portraits
- Mix 2: Head and shoulders — 50% of portraits
- Mix 3: Wider shot (chest up or environmental) — 20% of portraits
- The ratio can shift, but having ALL of one type creates monotony

**Negative space as the luxury signal:**
- Budget/mass-market: portraits fill every available pixel, no space wasted
- Premium/editorial: 20-30% of the total area is empty space
- Ultra-premium/gallery: 40-50% of the total area is empty space
- For Maven's "premium but not pretentious" positioning: aim for 20-25% negative space in the portrait grid area

### Specific Layout Compositions for 10-24 Portraits

**Layout A — "The Feature Wall" (for 10-12 portraits)**
```
[  HERO 3x3  ] [ med ] [ med ]
[             ] [ med ] [ med ]
[             ] [ med ] [ med ]
[ sm ] [ sm ] [ sm ] [ sm ]
```
One hero at top-left spanning 3 grid rows. 6 medium portraits in a 2x3 grid adjacent. 4 small portraits in a row below. Total: 11 portraits.

**Layout B — "The Magazine Spread" (for 12-16 portraits)**
```
[ Hero full-width bleed ]
[ sm ] [ sm ] [  med  ] [ sm ] [ sm ]
[ sm ] [  med  ] [ sm ] [ sm ] [ sm ]
```
Full-width featured portrait at top (50-60vh). Below: two rows with mixed sizes. Staggered alignment. Category label or text block replacing one grid cell per row.

**Layout C — "The Gallery Walk" (for 16-24 portraits)**
```
[ med ] [lg ] [ sm ] [ sm ] [med ]
[ sm  ] [   ] [ med] [ sm ] [sm  ]
[ med ] [sm ] [ sm ] [lg  ] [sm  ]
```
A more complex bento grid with deliberate size variation. Two "anchor" portraits at large scale, placed asymmetrically (not both in the same row or column). Remaining portraits fill at small and medium. One cell is a text/category element.

**Layout D — "The Constellation" (for 10-16 portraits)**
No grid at all. Portraits placed freely on the viewport, like stars in a constellation or pictures on a pinboard. Connected by subtle lines or unified by a background texture. The featured portrait is largest and central. Others radiate outward at varying distances and sizes. Feels organic and artistic but requires careful composition to avoid feeling random.

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Use exactly 3 portrait sizes: large (1), medium (3-5), small (remaining).** This creates clear hierarchy without chaos. The large portrait is the featured instructor for the active category.

2. **Stagger rows rather than perfectly aligning them.** Offset each row by 20-40px vertically. This single change is the fastest way to make a grid feel designed rather than generated.

3. **Replace one grid cell per row with a non-portrait element.** A category label, a pull quote ("Learn Decision Making from humans"), a colored block in Maven's category color, or simply an empty cell. These "breaks" prevent grid fatigue.

4. **Crop 30% of portraits tight (face only), 50% at head-and-shoulders, 20% wider.** This creates variety in a system that still feels cohesive because the photography treatment is consistent.

5. **Let edge portraits be cropped by the viewport.** Portraits at the edges of the viewport should be cut off — showing half a face, or a sliver of a shoulder. This implies the grid extends beyond the screen, communicating "there are even more instructors than what you see."

6. **Overlap portraits slightly at different depths.** Using z-index and slight position offsets, let some portraits sit 10-20px "behind" adjacent ones. This creates depth and makes the grid feel composed rather than stamped out.

## 7. Color + Photography

### Monochromatic Photo Treatments

**Duotone (two-color mapping).** The image's tonal range is mapped to two colors instead of black-and-white. Shadows become one color, highlights become another. For Maven: shadows mapped to `#080c28` (lapis-900 navy), highlights mapped to `#2465e8` (lapis-400 blue) or `#b2d3fa` (lapis-100 light blue). This creates an unmistakably branded image treatment while preserving all the tonal detail of the photograph.

**Tritone (three-color mapping).** Adds a third color for midtones. Shadows = navy, midtones = medium blue, highlights = light blue or white. More tonal range than duotone, but still cohesive. For Maven: navy/medium blue/white tritone would be elegant and on-brand.

**CSS/Canvas implementation of duotone:**
```
mix-blend-mode: multiply on a colored overlay (for shadows)
+ mix-blend-mode: screen on a second colored overlay (for highlights)
```
Or in Canvas 2D: sample each pixel, map its luminance to a gradient between two chosen colors.

**When to use monochromatic treatments:**
- When unifying diverse photography is the priority (different studios, lighting, backgrounds)
- When you want the layout/typography to be the star, not the photography itself
- When the brand color palette is strong enough to carry the visual identity
- When you want a "gallery catalog" or "editorial feature" feeling

**When NOT to use monochromatic treatments:**
- When the goal is "meet real humans" (duotone dehumanizes slightly — it aestheticizes)
- When the photography quality is already high and consistent
- When warmth and approachability are higher priorities than visual cohesion

### Colored Backgrounds Behind Portraits — When They Work vs. When They Feel Cheap

**When colored backgrounds WORK:**
- **Muted, desaturated tones.** Sage green, dusty rose, slate blue, warm grey. Maven's teach page uses this successfully — each instructor has a unique muted background.
- **One color per category.** AI instructors on deep purple, Product on burnt orange, Leadership on burgundy. The color becomes metadata, not decoration.
- **Low contrast between background and portrait.** The background should be close in value (brightness) to the portrait's tones. High-contrast backgrounds (bright yellow behind a dark portrait) feel like PowerPoint.
- **Consistent value range.** If using multiple background colors, they should all be similar in brightness. All pastels, or all deep/muted tones. Mixing bright and dark backgrounds breaks cohesion.

**When colored backgrounds feel CHEAP:**
- **Saturated primary colors.** Bright red, blue, yellow, green behind portraits = corporate template / stock photo site
- **Gradient backgrounds.** Unless extremely subtle, gradients behind portraits feel 2010s and dated
- **Different background styles per portrait.** One person on solid blue, another on a texture, another on a pattern = chaotic, undesigned
- **White or very light backgrounds in a dark-themed layout.** Creates visual "holes" in the composition. If the page is dark navy, portrait backgrounds should be dark tones too.

**The safest approaches for Maven:**
1. **Cut out all subjects, place on shared dark navy (`#080c28`).** Creates MasterClass-level consistency. The dark background is dramatic and the faces pop.
2. **Category-colored backgrounds at low saturation.** Take Maven's category colors and desaturate them 40-50%, darken them 30-40%. AI instructors on a very dark purple, Product on a very dark burnt orange. Subtle enough to not compete with the faces, distinct enough to group by category.
3. **Consistent light grey background.** Remove all original backgrounds, replace with a single light grey (`#e8e8e8` to `#d4d4d4`). This is the Irving Penn / Avedon approach — neutral, timeless, lets the faces be the color.

### Unifying Diverse Photography Styles into One Cohesive Grid

This is the most practical section. When you have 24 headshots from different photographers, different studios, different cameras, different years — here's how to make them feel like one series.

**Step 1 — Match the white balance.** The single most impactful adjustment. If some photos are warm (yellowish) and some are cool (bluish), the grid immediately looks incoherent. Pick a target color temperature (5500K daylight for neutral, 4800K for slightly cool/Maven blue-ish) and adjust all photos to match.

**Step 2 — Normalize the contrast curve.** Some photos may have deep blacks and blown highlights (high contrast), while others are flat and grey (low contrast). Apply a consistent contrast curve to all: lift the blacks slightly (RGB 0 maps to 15-20), keep midtones, and bring highlights down slightly (RGB 255 maps to 240-245). This creates the "editorial" look — rich but not harsh.

**Step 3 — Match the saturation.** Desaturate all photos by 15-25% from their originals. This pulls diverse color palettes toward a common, muted center. Strong reds become soft reds. Bright blues become steely blues. The effect is subtle but the cohesion is immediate.

**Step 4 — Unify the background.** Either: (a) remove all backgrounds and replace with one color, or (b) if keeping original backgrounds, ensure they all fall within the same tonal range (all light grey, all mid grey, all dark).

**Step 5 — Match the crop.** Even if you're using varied crops for visual rhythm, the aspect ratio of each image should come from a limited set (1:1 square, 3:4 portrait, 2:3 portrait). Don't mix squares with 16:9 rectangles with 4:5 portraits — keep to 2 aspect ratios maximum.

**Step 6 — Apply a subtle brand tint.** The final unifying layer: a very subtle color cast across all images. For Maven: shift the shadows 5-10% toward blue (`#080c28` direction). This is almost imperceptible on any individual image, but across a grid of 10-24 images, it creates a consistent "Maven blue" undertone that ties everything to the brand.

**CSS approach for consistent treatment:**
```css
.instructor-portrait {
  filter:
    saturate(0.8)        /* desaturate 20% */
    contrast(1.05)        /* slight contrast boost */
    brightness(0.95);     /* slight darkening */
}

/* Blue tint overlay */
.instructor-portrait::after {
  content: '';
  position: absolute;
  inset: 0;
  background: #080c28;
  mix-blend-mode: color;
  opacity: 0.08;  /* very subtle */
}
```

### Actionable Principles for Maven

1. **Process all 24 instructor headshots through the same 6-step pipeline** (white balance, contrast, saturation, background, crop, tint) before placing them in any layout. This is a prerequisite, not an option. Unprocessed diverse photos will undermine any layout.

2. **For the dark theme (navy `#080c28` background): cut out subjects and place on navy.** This is the highest-impact approach for a dark layout. The faces become the only color, and the dark background creates cinema/gallery atmosphere.

3. **For a lighter theme: use Irving Penn grey (`#d8d8d8`) as a universal background.** Neutral, timeless, lets the photography speak.

4. **Apply a subtle blue shift to all portrait shadows.** Not enough to look "filtered" — just enough to make the images feel like they belong to Maven's visual world. A 5-8% color overlay in `mix-blend-mode: color` at the CSS level.

5. **Avoid using Maven's category colors at full saturation as portrait backgrounds.** Desaturate and darken them significantly if using as backgrounds. At full saturation, they compete with the faces.

6. **Use duotone as a hover/transition state, not a default state.** Show portraits in full color by default. On hover or during category transitions, portraits could briefly shift to a blue duotone before revealing the next state. This creates a "digital/human" contrast that reinforces the "Learn from Humans" theme.

## Synthesis: Principles Ranked by Impact for Maven's Landing Page

### Tier 1 — Non-Negotiable (do these or the grid looks amateur)

1. **Consistent photography treatment.** All 24 portraits through the same color pipeline. Matched white balance, contrast, saturation, background. This is step zero.

2. **Size hierarchy.** One featured portrait at 2-3x the size of the others. The eye needs an anchor.

3. **Eye-line alignment.** Crop all portraits so eyes sit at approximately the same vertical position (33% from top). Creates invisible order.

4. **Adequate negative space.** 20-25% of the grid area should be breathing room. Gutters of 16-32px.

### Tier 2 — High Impact (elevates from "decent" to "premium")

5. **Broken grid / staggered rows.** Offset alternate rows by 20-40px. Transforms template into design.

6. **Crop variation.** 30% tight face, 50% head-and-shoulders, 20% wider. Creates rhythm.

7. **Break elements.** Replace one grid cell per row with typography, color block, or empty space. Prevents monotony.

8. **Edge cropping.** Portraits at viewport edges are cut off, implying the grid extends beyond view.

### Tier 3 — Distinguishing Details (separates "premium" from "award-winning")

9. **Overlapping depths.** Slight z-index variation creates layers and dimensional composition.

10. **Cursor-reactive microinteractions.** Subtle parallax or hover-reveal on the portrait grid.

11. **Asymmetric composition.** Hero text and portrait grid placed according to Swiss grid principles — not centered, not symmetrical.

12. **Category color as metadata.** Subtle color dots, thin borders, or background tints — not dominant blocks.

## Quick-Reference: The "Do / Don't" Grid

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Process all photos through same pipeline | Mix raw photos from different studios |
| Use 2-3 portrait sizes with clear hierarchy | Make all portraits the same size |
| Stagger/offset rows for rhythm | Perfectly align everything into a rigid grid |
| Crop some portraits at viewport edges | Contain all portraits within safe margins |
| Use consistent backgrounds (cut out or matched) | Mix different background colors/styles |
| Place text adjacent to portraits, not over faces | Overlay text on faces |
| Use muted/desaturated category colors | Use saturated primary colors as backgrounds |
| Let negative space breathe (20-25% of area) | Fill every pixel with content |
| Vary crops: tight face + head/shoulders + wider | Use identical crops for all portraits |
| Break the grid with text/color/empty cells | Run uninterrupted rows of identical portrait cells |
